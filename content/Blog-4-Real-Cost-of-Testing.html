The Real Cost of Testing: How Diagnostic Spend Adds Up in High-Volume and Rural Practice

Educational information from The Aesculapius Foundation. This article is non-commercial and independent — it recommends no products or vendors — and is not financial or veterinary advice.

Ask what a diagnostic test costs and most people name the price of the reagent or the send-out fee. That's the tip of the iceberg. The real cost of testing is a fuller picture — one that high-volume, low-cost, rural, and shelter practices feel acutely, because they run a lot of tests on tight margins. Seeing the whole cost is the first step to spending it well, and spending it well is not the same as spending less.

Spending well isn't the same as spending less.

The visible costs

These are the ones on the invoice: reagents and consumables, send-out lab fees, and the per-test price of in-house cartridges or slides. They're easy to see and easy to compare, which is why they get all the attention. But on their own they tell you surprisingly little about what a testing program actually costs your practice.

The hidden costs

Below the waterline sit the costs that rarely make it into a spreadsheet — and often dwarf the reagent price:

  • Staff time. Drawing, prepping, running, quality-control, interpreting, and recording. In a busy day, minutes per test add up to hours per week.
  • Equipment ownership. Not just the analyzer, but calibration, maintenance, service contracts, downtime, and the space it occupies.
  • Repeat and rework. Hemolyzed samples, ambiguous results, and confirmatory runs. A test you have to run twice costs far more than its label price.
  • Send-out logistics. Packaging, shipping, courier timing, and the workflow disruption of results that arrive a day or two later.
  • The cost of not testing. The hardest one to quantify and often the largest: a wrong or delayed decision, an avoidable treatment, or a missed diagnosis when the right test wasn't run at the right time.

The cheapest test is often the one you didn't have to run twice.

Reframing the question

Once the hidden costs are visible, the useful question stops being "which test is cheapest?" and becomes "which testing choice gets us the right answer, at the right time, with the least total cost and disruption?" Sometimes that's an in-house point-of-care test that returns a result during the visit; sometimes it's a send-out that's cheaper per unit but slower. The right answer depends on your caseload, your staffing, and what decision hangs on the result — not on the sticker price alone.

How to find your baseline

You don't need an accounting system to start — you need honest numbers on your most common tests. A five-minute exercise:

  • List the three diagnostics you run most often.
  • For each, estimate the visible cost (reagent or send-out fee) and the staff time it consumes.
  • Note how often each one gets repeated, and roughly why.
  • Ask: for each, does the result actually change what we do? If not, that's the first place to look.

Even rough figures tend to reveal a surprise or two — a "cheap" test that's expensive in labor, or a repeat rate worth fixing. Knowing your baseline is what turns cost management from guesswork into decisions.

Spending well, not just spending less

For mission-driven practices, this matters beyond the bottom line. Every dollar spent on a test that didn't change a decision is a dollar not available for the animal that needed it. Understanding true diagnostic cost isn't about doing less medicine — it's about directing limited resources to where they change outcomes. That's the whole point of thinking about cost at all.

This is also why the Foundation runs a Diagnostic Usage & Cost Survey — to build a shared, independent picture of what testing really costs in the field, so our free education and grant priorities reflect reality rather than assumption. If you run diagnostics, your (anonymous) numbers make the whole community's picture sharper.
