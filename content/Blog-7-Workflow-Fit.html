Choosing Diagnostics That Fit Your Workflow (Not Just Your Wish List)

Educational information from The Aesculapius Foundation. This article is non-commercial and independent — it recommends no products or vendors — and is not veterinary or purchasing advice.

Every practice has a diagnostic wish list: the analyzer with the longest menu, the fastest headline turnaround, the most impressive spec sheet. But the most capable instrument on paper is worthless if it sits unused in a corner because it doesn't fit how your team actually works. The better question isn't "what's the best test?" It's "what's the best test for us?"

A great test in the wrong workflow is a bad test.

The wish-list trap

Spec sheets are written to impress, not to predict daily reality. They rarely tell you how a test behaves at 4pm on a fully booked day, who will actually run it, or how often results need repeating. Buying to the spec sheet is how practices end up with capable equipment that quietly becomes a very expensive paperweight. Buying to your workflow is how you get a tool your team reaches for every day.

Five questions that predict real-world fit

1. Turnaround — in real life

Not the best-case number on the brochure, but the realistic time from sample to actionable result during a normal shift, including prep and cleanup. A result that arrives while the patient is still in the room is worth far more than one that's technically "fast" but lands after they've gone home.

2. Who runs it, and how much training does it take?

A test is only as reliable as its least-trained regular operator on a busy day. Consider staff turnover, onboarding time, and how forgiving the workflow is of small errors. Simple and reliable usually beats sophisticated and fragile.

3. Space, throughput, and maintenance

Does it fit your physical space and caseload? What does upkeep demand — calibration, quality control, service, downtime? These recurring realities shape total cost and daily usability more than the purchase price does.

4. Validation for your patients and samples

Performance is not universal. A test validated in one species, sample type, or setting may behave differently in yours. Look for evidence that a test performs well for the animals and specimens you actually handle — and weigh independent, peer-reviewed validation more heavily than marketing claims. (More on that below.)

5. Total value, not sticker price

As we covered in our piece on the real cost of testing, the meaningful figure is total cost — reagents plus staff time, upkeep, repeats, and the cost of a wrong or delayed call — measured against how often the result actually changes a decision. Fit and value, not features, should drive the choice.

Fit beats features.

The two-week fit test

Before committing, trial the workflow if you can. For two weeks, track three things: the real turnaround you observe, how often the process hiccups or needs a repeat, and whether the team actually reaches for the tool without prompting. Let daily reality — not the brochure — make the final call. What people use is worth more than what looks best on paper.

A word on performance claims

Vendor materials are a starting point, not a verdict. Wherever possible, look for independent, peer-reviewed evaluations of a diagnostic's performance — studies conducted without a commercial stake in the result, ideally in conditions and species like yours. Independent validation is exactly the kind of evidence the Foundation exists to help surface and translate, so that purchasing decisions rest on data rather than persuasion. When independent evidence is thin, treat strong claims with appropriate caution and lean harder on your own trial.

A simple decision guide

  • Start from the decisions you need to make, and work backward to the test.
  • Score candidates on turnaround, training, space/maintenance, validation for your patients, and total value.
  • Prefer independent evidence over marketing for performance claims.
  • Trial the workflow before you commit.
  • Choose the tool your team will reliably use — that's the one that improves care.
