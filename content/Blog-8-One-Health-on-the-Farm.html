One Health on the Farm: Protecting People, Livestock, and the Environment Together

Educational information from The Aesculapius Foundation. This article is not veterinary or medical advice.

One Health can sound like an abstract policy phrase — human, animal, and environmental health as one interconnected system. On a working farm, it stops being abstract. The people, the animals, and the land share the same water, the same air, the same soil, and often the same hands at the end of a long day. Protect one carelessly and you put the others at risk; protect them thoughtfully and the same everyday habits guard all three at once.

On a farm, people, animals, and the land aren't three separate health problems. They're one.

The three circles, on one piece of ground

A farm makes the connections concrete. A virus in wild birds reaches the flock (environment to animal). A pathogen in cattle reaches the worker who milks them (animal to human). Runoff and manure carry organisms and resistance genes into soil and water (animal to environment, and back again). None of these travels in a straight line, and none respects the fence line — which is exactly why farm health is best managed as a whole rather than one problem at a time.

Everyday steps that protect all three

The encouraging part: most protective habits do double or triple duty. A handful of routines cover a lot of ground.

Wound care and fly control

Prompt attention to wounds and good fly management protect animal welfare, reduce parasite and disease pressure — including New World screwworm during fly season — and keep small problems from becoming big ones. Check newborns and fresh wounds routinely.

Biosecurity basics

Limiting visitors, dedicating and cleaning footwear and equipment, keeping wild birds away from feed and water, and isolating new or sick animals are the front line against HPAI and many other diseases. Consistency matters more than complexity — the routine you can maintain beats the elaborate plan you can't.

Hygiene and worker protection

Handwashing, appropriate protective equipment when handling sick animals or birthing, and a low threshold for seeking care when a worker feels unwell protect people directly — and people are part of the system, not bystanders to it.

Water, waste, and the environment

Sensible manure and water management protects the land and downstream neighbors, and reduces the environmental reservoir that can cycle pathogens and resistance back to animals and people.

Thoughtful antimicrobial use

Using antimicrobials only when indicated, guided by veterinary advice and diagnostics where appropriate, protects the animals now and preserves effective treatment for the future — a One Health benefit that reaches far beyond the farm.

Protect one, protect all

The through-line is simple: on a farm, the healthiest choices are usually the ones that help people, animals, and the environment simultaneously. That's not extra work layered on top of good farming — it is good farming, viewed through a slightly wider lens.

Working with your veterinarian

Your veterinarian is the natural quarterback of farm One Health — connecting herd health, worker considerations, and environmental practices, and knowing which signs warrant a report and to whom. A standing relationship, not just a crisis call, is one of the best investments a farm can make.

Reporting & sources

  • Suspected animal disease: your veterinarian, your state animal health official, or USDA/APHIS (aphis.usda.gov).
  • Screwworm response hub: screwworm.gov.
  • Human illness linked to animals: seek medical care and contact your local or state health department; general information at cdc.gov.
