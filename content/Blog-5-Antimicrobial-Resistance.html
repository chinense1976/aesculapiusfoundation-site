Antimicrobial Resistance Starts in the Clinic: Stewardship & Surveillance Basics

Educational information from The Aesculapius Foundation. This article is not veterinary or medical advice, and it recommends no specific medication. Prescribing decisions belong with the treating clinician.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a slow-motion One Health emergency. It rarely makes a dramatic headline, but it quietly erodes something we depend on every day: the ability to treat infection with a reliable drug. And while it can feel like a distant, global problem, a meaningful part of it begins somewhere very ordinary — the exam room, with a single prescribing decision.

Resistance doesn't start in a distant hospital. It starts with the next prescription.

What AMR is, and why it crosses every boundary

When bacteria are exposed to an antimicrobial, the ones that happen to survive go on to multiply. Over time — and with every unnecessary or mismatched course — resistant strains become more common, until a once-dependable drug stops working. What makes this a One Health issue rather than only a medical one is that resistant bacteria and resistance genes don't respect species lines. They move among animals, people, and the shared environment: through direct contact, through food and water systems, through manure and soil. Resistance that emerges in one setting can surface in another far away. Human medicine, veterinary medicine, and environmental health are, once again, one system.

Diagnostics are a stewardship tool

Here's where the exam room comes in. The single most powerful lever against AMR is using antimicrobials well — and diagnostics are what make "well" possible. Culture and susceptibility testing, used at the right moment, turns a guess into a decision: is this even a bacterial infection, which organism is it, and which drugs will actually work against it? A well-timed diagnostic can mean the difference between a targeted, effective course and a broad-spectrum shot in the dark that fuels resistance without helping the patient.

That doesn't mean testing before every prescription — clinical judgment and context matter, and some situations warrant empirical treatment. It means recognizing diagnostics as an active part of stewardship, not an afterthought, especially for infections that are recurrent, severe, unusual, or not responding as expected.

Stewardship is about the right drug — not just fewer drugs

Stewardship isn't about using fewer drugs. It's about using the right one, at the right time, for the right length of time.

Antimicrobial stewardship is sometimes misheard as "use less." A better framing is "use right": the appropriate drug, at the appropriate dose, for the appropriate duration, only when it's genuinely indicated — and preserving the most critical drugs for when they're truly needed. Every well-chosen prescription is a small One Health win. Practical habits that support this include confirming a bacterial cause before reaching for an antimicrobial, choosing the narrowest effective option, completing appropriate courses, and revisiting empirical choices once results return.

How resistance is tracked: surveillance

You can't manage what you don't measure, and AMR is measured through coordinated surveillance. In the United States, the National Antimicrobial Resistance Monitoring System (NARMS) — a partnership among the CDC, FDA, and USDA — tracks resistance in bacteria across people, animals, and the food supply, which is itself a One Health design. Globally, organizations including the World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH) and the World Health Organization coordinate monitoring and response. These systems turn scattered local observations into a picture the whole system can act on — another reason accurate diagnostics and reporting at the clinic level matter beyond the individual case.

What you can do

  • Treat diagnostics as part of prescribing, particularly for recurrent, severe, or non-responsive infections.
  • Favor the narrowest effective option guided by susceptibility data where available.
  • Educate the whole team and clients — realistic expectations reduce pressure for unnecessary antimicrobials.
  • Support surveillance by reporting and record-keeping that feed the larger picture.

AMR can feel too big to influence from a single practice. But the aggregate of countless everyday decisions is exactly what drives — or slows — resistance. Stewardship is how the clinic becomes part of the solution.
