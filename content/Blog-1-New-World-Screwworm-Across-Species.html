New World Screwworm Is Back in the U.S.: What Early Detection Looks Like Across Species

Educational information from The Aesculapius Foundation. This article explains what to watch for and who to contact. It is not veterinary or medical advice.

For the first time in decades, New World screwworm has been confirmed in animals inside the United States. On June 3, 2026, the U.S. Department of Agriculture confirmed a case in a three-week-old calf in Zavala County, Texas, and additional cases have since been confirmed across Texas and New Mexico — in cattle, a dog, and a goat. It is a development worth understanding calmly and clearly, because the single most powerful tool against screwworm is something almost anyone can do: notice an unusual wound early, and make one phone call.

This is not only a livestock story. Screwworm can affect nearly any warm-blooded animal — cattle, dogs, cats, horses, wildlife — and, rarely, people. That is exactly why early detection is a shared responsibility, and why we are writing this for the whole community rather than one audience.

Early detection doesn't require a lab. It requires someone who knows what to look for — and who to call.

What New World screwworm actually is

New World screwworm is a parasitic fly whose larvae feed on the living tissue of animals. A female fly lays eggs at the edge of a wound — even a small one, like a tick bite, a scrape, or a fresh surgical or castration site — and when the larvae hatch, they burrow into healthy flesh. Left unaddressed, the wound enlarges quickly and attracts more flies, which is why speed matters so much.

The reassuring context: the U.S. food supply remains safe. Screwworm does not infest meat, fruit, vegetables, or other food products. The risk to people is low and is limited to areas where cases are occurring. And the United States has eradicated screwworm before — the coordinated response now underway is built on a proven playbook.

Why now — and why preparedness, not alarm

Screwworm re-established itself across Central America and Mexico over the past couple of years before reaching the U.S. border region in 2026. Federal and state animal-health agencies had been preparing for exactly this possibility, and the response — surveillance, movement controls, and the release of sterile flies to collapse the wild population — began immediately. Understanding that response helps explain why your role matters: every case that gets reported quickly tells responders precisely where to concentrate their efforts.

The signs to watch — across species

The underlying signs are the same everywhere: a wound that drains, enlarges, or seems to be "moving," and the presence of maggots in a wound or around body openings (the nose, ears, genitalia, or the navel of a newborn). What changes is where those signs tend to show up in each kind of animal.

Cattle and small ruminants

Newborns are especially vulnerable at the navel, so check calves, lambs, and kids in the days after birth. Also watch castration, branding, and dehorning sites, and any tick-bite lesions. A wound that looks worse the next day — rather than better — deserves immediate attention.

Dogs and cats

A dog was among the early confirmed U.S. cases, so companion-animal caregivers are part of surveillance too. Check healing wounds, surgical incisions, tick-bite spots, and the ears and genital area. The everyday rule is simple: a sore that won't heal, or that starts to drain or grow, is worth a call to your veterinarian.

Horses

Horses accumulate wounds, and any open wound is a potential site during fly season. Daily wound checks, good fly control, and prompt cleaning of injuries are ordinary horsemanship steps that double as screwworm prevention. Don't dismiss a lesion that is enlarging or draining.

Wildlife and the environment

Wild animals can carry and sustain screwworm, which is why the federal response includes wildlife surveillance. If you spend time outdoors or manage land and notice an animal with an unusual, maggot-filled wound, that observation is worth reporting to authorities. Healthy ecosystems are part of disease control.

People (rare, but worth knowing)

Human cases are uncommon, and the general risk remains low. Still, anyone who notices a suspicious, non-healing wound — particularly after time spent around affected animals or in an area with active cases — should seek medical care and mention the possibility to their clinician.

One pest, many species. The warning sign is the same everywhere: a wound that drains, enlarges, or seems to be moving.

Who to call if you suspect a case

Reporting is fast, free, and genuinely useful — it is how small outbreaks stay small. If you suspect screwworm:

  • Contact your veterinarian first. They can examine the animal and initiate reporting.
  • Contact your state animal health official or USDA/APHIS to report a suspected animal case.
  • In Texas, report suspected screwworm in livestock to the Texas Animal Health Commission (TAHC).
  • For a suspected human case, seek medical care and contact your local or state health department.

Treatment options exist that did not a year ago — the FDA has authorized products for several species — but treatment should always be guided by your veterinarian. The Foundation does not recommend or endorse any specific product; we simply want you to know that help is available and that your veterinarian is the right person to direct it.

How the national response works

The centerpiece of screwworm control is the sterile insect technique: releasing large numbers of sterile male flies so that wild females cannot produce viable offspring, steadily shrinking the population. Alongside that, agencies maintain heightened surveillance and trapping, movement controls around confirmed cases, and wildlife monitoring. It is a coordinated, science-based effort — and community reporting is one of its most important inputs.

What you can do this week

  • Learn the two signs: non-healing or draining wounds, and maggots in or around wounds and body openings.
  • Save the contacts: your veterinarian, your state animal health official, and the official hub at screwworm.gov.
  • Make it routine: add a quick wound check to newborn care and fly-season handling.
  • Tell one person: a colleague, a neighbor with backyard animals, a friend who spends time outdoors. The person who spots the first case is often not a professional.

Preparedness here is not fear — it is practiced attention. Knowing what to look for, and knowing who to call, is what turns an ordinary observer into part of an early-warning network that protects animals, people, and communities alike.
